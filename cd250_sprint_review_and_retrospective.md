# Sprint Review and Retrospective
- The SNHU Travel project demonstrated how a Scrum-Agile approach can help a development team respond to changing requirements while still producing a working product increment. Throughout the project, the team used Scrum roles, user stories, Product Backlog items, test cases, communication samples, and development work to move from broad travel-business goals toward a more specific travel slideshow application. The project was interrupted when the company changed direction and decided to focus on detox and wellness travel, but the Scrum-Agile process made that change manageable. Overall, the project showed that Agile is most effective when the team uses clear roles, visible tools, frequent communication, and incremental delivery.
Applying Scrum Roles. Each Scrum-Agile role contributed to the success of the SNHU Travel project in a different way. The Product Owner was responsible for representing business and user needs, interpreting stakeholder feedback, and prioritizing the Product Backlog.
- In this project, Christy acted as the Product Owner by gathering client and user feedback and translating that information into priorities for the team. When management decided that detox and wellness travel should become the focus of the product, the Product Owner helped clarify that the change was primarily about the types of travel and vacation packages being presented, not necessarily a complete rebuild of the product.
The Scrum Master’s role was important because the project needed structure, communication, and process support.
- As Scrum Master, I would focus on making sure the team understood the goal of the sprint, had access to the information needed to continue, and could identify obstacles before they became larger problems. The Scrum Master does not control the team’s work, but supports the team by helping Scrum events stay useful and by encouraging transparency.
The development role contributed by turning user stories and requirements into a working product increment. In the SNHU Travel assignments, this included modifying the slideshow so that it reflected the updated detox and wellness travel direction. The developer’s work depended on receiving clear requirements from the Product Owner and clear expectations from the tester. The tester contributed by turning requirements and user stories into test cases that could confirm whether the product met expectations. This role was especially important because a feature is not complete simply because code has been changed. It must also be verified against the intended behavior. Together, these roles helped the team move from business goals to user stories, test cases, and working software.

### Completing User Stories
- The Scrum-Agile approach helped user stories come to completion by breaking broad requirements into smaller pieces of work that could be prioritized, implemented, and tested. Instead of trying to complete the entire SNHU Travel product at once, the team could focus on individual user needs and product features. User stories helped connect the work to the user’s perspective by describing what the user wanted and why it mattered.
  - For example, the project involved travel customers who needed to view destination information in a clear and useful way. The Product Backlog helped organize those needs into work items. Test cases then helped define whether those work items had been completed successfully. This process supported completion because the team could connect each user story to acceptance criteria, development tasks, and testing expectations.
- The slideshow assignment is a good example of this process. The work was not simply “make a slideshow.” The product needed to present destinations in a way that matched the revised product direction. Once the focus changed to detox and wellness travel, the user stories and backlog priorities helped the team understand which content needed to change. The developer could then update the product increment, and the tester could verify whether the updated slideshow still worked as expected. This reflects an Agile approach to the SDLC because the team delivered work incrementally and adjusted the product as the requirements became clearer.

### Handling Interruptions and Change
- The strongest example of Agile supporting project completion occurred when the project was interrupted by a change in direction. SNHU Travel originally focused on general travel, but management later identified detox and wellness travel as an important market direction. This created requirement volatility because the team had already begun working from an earlier understanding of the product. Requirement volatility is common in software development, especially when business priorities, market research, or stakeholder feedback change during the project (Mohammad & Kollamana, 2024).
  - A traditional waterfall approach would have struggled more with this change because waterfall depends on requirements being stable early in the project. In contrast, Scrum-Agile expects change and provides a way to respond to it. The Product Owner could reprioritize the Product Backlog, the team could clarify the new expectations, and the developer could adapt the existing slideshow rather than starting from nothing. This did not make the interruption effortless, but it made the interruption manageable.
- In this case, the team did not need to discard all previous work. The existing slideshow structure could still be useful, but the content, destination choices, and acceptance criteria needed to align with the new detox and wellness focus. This showed one of the main strengths of Agile: it allows a team to inspect the current product, adapt to new information, and continue delivering value even when the project changes direction.

### Communication Samples and Collaboration
- Effective communication was necessary because the project change could easily have caused confusion. A vague instruction such as “make the project about wellness travel” would not be enough for a developer or tester to continue confidently. The team needed clarification about scope, priorities, and testing expectations.
  - One communication sample I used in the developer role was a clarification email to the Product Owner and tester. The message included questions such as:

> *“Can you please confirm whether this change is limited to the slideshow content, images, and destination descriptions, or whether any functionality should also change?”*

Another important question was:

> *“Can you please confirm whether the existing slideshow test cases can still be reused after the content update?”*

- These communication examples were effective because they were specific, directed to the correct team members, and connected to the work that needed to be completed. The first question asked the Product Owner to clarify scope so the developer would know whether to change only content or also functionality. The second question asked the tester to clarify which test cases still applied and which ones needed revision. This encouraged collaboration because it treated communication as part of the work itself, not as an extra task separate from development.
- Written communication was especially useful because it created a record the team could refer back to later. Dennis, Barlow, and Dennis (2022) found that text-based computer-mediated communication can support team performance in some virtual-team contexts, particularly because it allows team members to reflect, review, and contribute without relying only on live verbal discussion. In the SNHU Travel project, written clarification helped reduce ambiguity and supported collaboration among the Product Owner, developer, and tester.

### Organizational Tools and Scrum Events
- Several organizational tools and Scrum-Agile principles helped the team stay organized. The Product Backlog was one of the most important tools because it provided a prioritized list of work. During Sprint Planning, the Product Backlog would help the team decide which user stories and tasks should be included in the sprint. When the project changed direction, the backlog also gave the Product Owner a way to reprioritize work instead of allowing the change to remain vague.
- User stories were another important tool because they connected tasks to user value. They helped the team understand not only what needed to be built, but why the feature mattered. Test cases supported the Definition of Done by turning requirements into observable pass/fail checks. This helped the team avoid assuming that development was complete before testing confirmed the expected behavior.
- A Scrum board or project-management dashboard would also support the team by acting as an information radiator. During the Daily Scrum, the team could use the board to see what was in progress, what was blocked, and what had been completed. During the Sprint Review, the team could compare the completed increment against the sprint goal and stakeholder expectations. During the Sprint Retrospective, the team could reflect on what went well and what should improve in the next sprint.
- Tools such as Jira or Azure Boards could make this process more efficient by keeping user stories, tasks, bugs, owners, priorities, and status updates visible in one place. Ben Hassen and Bellaaj (2026) describe how AI, automation, and business intelligence can extend Jira with ticket automation, notifications, and real-time dashboards. While the SNHU Travel project did not require a complex tool integration, the principle is useful: project-management tools help teams coordinate because they make work visible between Scrum events.

### Evaluating the Agile Process
- The Scrum-Agile approach had several strengths during the SNHU Travel project. First, it made roles clear. The Product Owner focused on value and priorities, the Scrum Master supported the process, the developer implemented the product increment, and the tester verified whether the work met expectations. Second, Agile supported incremental progress. The team could work on specific user stories, produce a functional increment, and adjust based on feedback. Third, Agile helped the team handle changing requirements. When the project shifted toward detox and wellness travel, the team could adapt the existing work instead of treating the change as a total failure.
- Another strength was that Scrum-Agile encouraged communication. The project required clarification between the Product Owner, developer, and tester. This helped reduce ambiguity and made it more likely that the product would match the updated expectations. Scrum events such as Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, and Sprint Retrospective also supported communication by giving the team regular opportunities to inspect progress and adapt.
  - However, the Scrum-Agile approach also had some limitations. Agile does not automatically solve unclear requirements. If the Product Owner receives ambiguous feedback or does not clarify priorities, the team can still experience confusion and rework. Agile also depends on team members communicating honestly about blockers and progress. If team members do not update the board, ask for help, or raise concerns during Scrum events, problems can remain hidden. Another limitation is that frequent change can still create stress, even when the process supports adaptation. Agile makes requirement changes easier to manage, but it does not make them cost-free.
- For the SNHU Travel project, Scrum-Agile was the best approach. The project involved changing priorities, evolving user needs, and a significant shift in direction after development work had already begun. A waterfall approach would have been less effective because it would have depended more heavily on complete requirements at the beginning of the project. Scrum-Agile was a better fit because the team could refine the backlog, clarify requirements, update the increment, and continue working toward a useful product.

### Conclusion
- The SNHU Travel project showed that Scrum-Agile is most effective when the team uses its roles, artifacts, events, and communication practices together. The Product Owner helped define value and priorities, the Scrum Master supported the team process, the developer created the product increment, and the tester verified that the work met expectations. User stories, backlogs, test cases, and project-management tools helped organize the work and make progress visible. When the project changed direction toward detox and wellness travel, Agile supported completion by allowing the team to adapt instead of starting over. The biggest lesson from the project is that Agile does not eliminate change or ambiguity, but it gives the team a practical structure for responding to both.
